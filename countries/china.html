China

Historical

Christianity entered China in the Tang dynasty through Nestorian missionaries, endured suppression during the Ming and Qing dynasties, and experienced periodic anti-Christian violence during the Boxer Rebellion (1899–1901), during which many missionaries and converts were killed. Catholic and Protestant missions expanded in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, only to be curtailed with the establishment of the People's Republic in 1949. The Cultural Revolution (1966–1976) targeted churches, destroyed religious sites, and incarcerated clergy.

Modern

China ranks among the world’s most restrictive environments for Christians. The government controls official state-approved churches through the Three-Self Patriotic Movement and the Chinese Catholic Patriotic Association, while unregistered “house churches” face crackdowns, surveillance, and demolition. Under recent regulations, unregistered religious personnel may be punished under administrative law. Outreach to minors and online religious content are restricted. Authorities frequently detain leaders and members of independent churches.
